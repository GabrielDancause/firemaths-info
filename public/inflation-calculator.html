What is Inflation?

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, eroding purchasing power. As inflation occurs, every dollar you own buys a smaller percentage of a good or service. This means your money's value decreases over time, requiring you to spend more dollars tomorrow to purchase what you could buy with fewer dollars today.

Central banks typically attempt to limit inflation, and avoid deflation, in order to keep the economy running smoothly. For personal finance planning, understanding historical inflation is critical because it highlights why holding cash long-term is generally a poor strategy compared to investing in assets that outpace inflation.

How the Consumer Price Index (CPI) Works

This calculator uses data from the Consumer Price Index (CPI), specifically the CPI-U (Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers), which is provided by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS). The CPI measures the average change over time in the prices paid by urban consumers for a market basket of consumer goods and services.

  • Housing and Groceries: The CPI basket includes common everyday expenses such as housing, food, transportation, and medical care.
  • Historical Context: Our data spans back to 1913, when the BLS first started tracking these metrics consistently.
  • Purchasing Power: By comparing the CPI of a past year to the CPI of a more recent year, we can accurately determine how much buying power a specific amount of money has gained or lost.

Why You Must Plan for Inflation in FIRE

When pursuing Financial Independence, Retire Early (FIRE), inflation is one of the most significant variables. If your expenses are $40,000 today, an average inflation rate of just 3% means you will need nearly $72,000 to maintain the same standard of living in 20 years.

The famous "4% Rule" (based on the Trinity Study) implicitly accounts for inflation by assuming your portfolio will grow at an inflation-adjusted rate, allowing you to increase your withdrawal amount each year to match rising costs. Failing to factor inflation into your retirement numbers often leads to drastically underestimating your target FIRE number.
