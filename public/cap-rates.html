Methodology

This study collected data for 50 of the largest US cities. Median home values and average monthly gross rents were aggregated using recent US Census ACS data.

Calculations:

  • Annual Rent: Average Monthly Rent × 12
  • Net Operating Income (NOI): Annual Rent - Estimated Expenses (calculated flat at 45% of gross rent).
  • Cap Rate: (NOI / Median Property Price) × 100
  • Price-to-Rent Ratio: Median Property Price / Annual Rent

Note: Data fields marked as 'N/A' indicate insufficient public data points for that specific municipality. Values are estimates and do not constitute financial advice.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good cap rate for a rental property in 2026?

A "good" cap rate depends on the market, property class, and your investment strategy. Generally, investors look for cap rates between 4% and 8%. Lower cap rates typically indicate lower risk (e.g., Class A properties in major metros), while higher cap rates indicate higher risk or emerging markets.

How is the capitalization rate calculated in this study?

The cap rate is calculated as: (Annual Rent - Estimated Expenses) / Median Property Price. In this study, we conservatively estimate operating expenses at 45% of gross annual rent, which accounts for property taxes, insurance, maintenance, vacancy, and management fees.

Why do some major cities have such low cap rates?

Major metropolitan areas like New York and San Francisco often have very low cap rates (sometimes under 3%). This is because high property values outpace rental income. Investors in these markets usually prioritize long-term property appreciation over immediate cash flow.

What is the Price-to-Rent ratio?

The price-to-rent ratio is calculated by dividing the median home price by the median annual rent. A lower ratio (under 15) suggests it is better to buy than rent, and typically indicates a strong market for cash-flow investors. A high ratio (over 20) indicates renting is relatively cheaper than buying.

Where does this real estate data come from?

The data in this study is synthesized from public US Census ACS data for median home values and gross rents. Because markets fluctuate rapidly, this data serves as a baseline for comparing relative market strength rather than an absolute guarantee of current neighborhood-level pricing.
